Citymart expands to U.S. to make procurement more open, agile and resilient<\/h4>\n

by Sascha Haselmayer<\/p>\n

\n
\n

Knight Foundation<\/a> supports Citymart\u2019s U.S. launch to help Knight communities<\/a>experience a new approach to public procurement. Below, Sascha Haselmayer<\/a>, founder and CEO of Citymart<\/a>, writes about the benefits of a more open approach to the procurement process.<\/em><\/p>\n

Take a moment and visit your city\u2019s website. Look up the section on \u201cProblems we plan to solve\u201d or \u201cSubmit your proposals here if your solution can deliver a better service at lower cost.\u201d<\/p>\n

You didn\u2019t find it? Don\u2019t worry; it likely doesn\u2019t exist, and that is just how it works almost everywhere today. That\u2019s a missed opportunity.<\/p>\n

For several years we\u2019ve been working with cities around the world about how to change that, and new support from Knight Foundation<\/a> will help broaden this work in the United States.<\/p>\n

If you search your city\u2019s website, what you probably will find are policy announcements or a strategic plan for your community. You may even find the page where your city publishes requests for proposals, or RFPs, which are the public contracts companies can bid for.<\/p>\n

Dig further into one of those RFPs, and you will find dozens, if not hundreds of pages of specifications, terms and conditions, and legal clauses. You will belong to the chosen few if you can make out what it is your community wants to buy. You will also notice that if you have a better solution than what is asked for, it is too late. Since the city didn\u2019t publish the problem earlier, there was no way for you to educate them about your offering.<\/p>\n

Welcome to the world of public procurement. Professionals use adjectives such as \u201crigid,\u201d \u201cconfrontational,\u201d \u201cabstract\u201d and \u201crisk-averse\u201d to describe it. That\u2019s a shame because public procurement by local governments constitute the world\u2019s single largest budget to improve communities \u2013 estimated at $1 trillion annually just in the U.S.<\/p>\n
